# Wireframe Catalog

Choose one wireframe before building. The wireframe is a marketer-facing plan, not buyer-facing copy.

## Selection Rules

- Match the campaign goal first.
- Match the audience and campaign style second.
- Use the available proof, resources, and approved materials honestly.
- Do not choose a proof-led or stat-led structure unless the proof is real and approved.
- Avoid a generic hero plus three equal cards unless that is the best fit for the campaign and source brand.
- Include only interactions that can be built and QAed with real behavior.

## Wireframes

### ABM Account Microsite

Best for one named account or a small named account cluster.

Typical structure:

1. Account-specific first viewport with vendor promise and account context
2. Why this matters now
3. Business priority or operational challenge
4. Solution path
5. Role-specific value
6. Proof and resources
7. Workshop, meeting, or evaluation CTA

Use when the marketer has account context and wants a tailored buyer journey.

Avoid when there is no account-specific input or the page should be broadly reusable.

### Demand Generation Landing Page

Best for paid, email, webinar, content syndication, or campaign traffic.

Typical structure:

1. Offer-led first viewport
2. Problem and urgency
3. What the visitor gets
4. Proof or credibility
5. Form, meeting, or resource CTA
6. Related resources or next steps

Use when the main job is conversion.

Avoid when the audience needs a deep account-specific story.

### Resource Hub

Best for nurture, sales follow-up, or multi-asset journeys.

Typical structure:

1. Audience-specific promise
2. Featured resource
3. Browsable content paths
4. Persona, industry, or maturity filters
5. Proof or case-study band
6. Recommended next step

Use when several assets should be organized into a guided path.

Avoid when the page has only one offer.

### Event Follow-Up

Best after webinars, field events, trade shows, executive dinners, or workshops.

Typical structure:

1. Event-specific recap and next step
2. Session or theme highlights
3. Resources promised at the event
4. Choose-your-path modules by interest
5. Proof or customer examples
6. Book follow-up or continue the conversation

Use when attendees need a clean post-event destination.

Avoid when the page should not reference the event.

### Product Launch

Best for new capability, release, integration, or product story.

Typical structure:

1. Launch message and audience promise
2. What changed
3. Why it matters
4. Capability tour
5. Proof, demo, or use-case examples
6. Launch CTA

Use when the product story is the center of the campaign.

Avoid when the campaign is primarily account-specific or resource-led.

### Executive Briefing

Best for senior audiences and strategic account plays.

Typical structure:

1. Executive-level thesis
2. Market, account, or business pressure
3. Strategic path
4. Risk and value tradeoffs
5. Proof and operating model
6. Executive workshop or briefing CTA

Use when clarity and credibility matter more than many interactions.

Avoid when the audience wants tactical product detail first.

### Role-Based Chooser

Best for buying committees with distinct needs.

Typical structure:

1. Shared campaign promise
2. Role selector or persona path
3. Role-specific pain, proof, and resources
4. Shared proof
5. CTA matched to role or common next step

Use when economic buyers, practitioners, IT, operations, and end users need different reasons to care.

Avoid when there is not enough role-specific content to avoid repetition.

### Comparison Or Evaluation Path

Best for competitive, replacement, renewal, or vendor evaluation campaigns.

Typical structure:

1. Evaluation promise
2. Current-state challenge
3. Criteria or decision framework
4. Comparison-safe differentiators
5. Proof and migration path
6. Evaluation workshop or demo CTA

Use when buyers need confidence in a decision path.

Avoid making unverified competitor claims.

### Interactive Workbench

Best for complex SaaS, platform, security, data, finance, workflow, or operational products.

Typical structure:

1. Outcome-led hero
2. Interactive scenario, selector, calculator, map, or workflow
3. What changes for each team or process
4. Proof and implementation path
5. Resources and CTA

Use when the product value is easiest to understand by interacting with the workflow.

Avoid when the interaction would be decorative or difficult to QA.
