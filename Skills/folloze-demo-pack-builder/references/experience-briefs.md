# Experience Briefs

Use this reference to draft the three briefs before handing each board to `Folloze-MCP-Demo-Builder`.

## Pack-Wide Brief

Create a compact pack-wide brief first:

- Company name, domain, category, product lines, and disambiguation notes
- Brand/source DNA summary and where the full harvest or notes live
- Public-source claim set and proof inventory
- Internal-planning-only inputs, if any, with redaction or source-boundary notes
- Pack promise: why these three examples belong together
- Shared visual constraints, button patterns, asset rules, and proof standards
- Save intent and theme-mode status

The pack-wide brief is not buyer-facing copy. It is the control layer for the three separate experiences.

## Campaign Landing Page Brief

Purpose: show a launchable one-to-many or one-to-few campaign the company could actually use.

Required decisions:

- Campaign audience or segment
- Singular solution spine or campaign promise
- Buyer tension and reason to act now
- Offer: meeting, workshop, assessment, guide, webinar, trial, consultation, or resource path
- Proof mix: customer story, logo proof, video, analyst/resource proof, benchmark, or product evidence
- CTA hierarchy and real destinations/actions
- Experience shape from `Folloze-MCP-Demo-Builder`

Quality gates:

- The first viewport reads like a real vendor campaign, not a page outline.
- Section headers are buyer-facing claims, not taxonomy or internal scaffolding.
- Customer stories use real proof assets when available.
- Every CTA does real work.
- The page has one main argument; product cards and resources support that argument.

## One-To-One Account Page Brief

Purpose: show how the company could make a focused ABM case to one named account or a tightly defined account segment.

Required decisions:

- Target account or segment and why it is credible
- Public account signals that matter to the buying motion
- Account-specific buyer priority
- Why change, why now, vendor promise, proof, and next action
- Role-specific decision support for leaders, practitioners, IT/security/ops, revenue, customer, learner, or employee teams as relevant
- Public proof that makes the account-specific case believable

Quality gates:

- The page feels like the vendor is speaking to the account, not like an agent explaining an account selection.
- No private intent, engagement, CRM, Gmail, Slack, Granola, or meeting-note details appear in buyer-facing copy unless explicitly user-approved.
- If the account can be swapped without weakening the copy, the brief is not specific enough.
- Public proof sits close to the main claim.
- The next action helps the buyer make a confident selection or alignment decision.

## Event Promotion Page Brief

Purpose: show how the company could promote attendance, meeting booking, registration, follow-up, or content engagement around a real event or event-adjacent campaign.

Source order:

1. Official company event page, webinar page, conference page, field event page, or registration page
2. Official company recap, blog post, press release, resource page, or video tied to an event
3. Partner or industry event page where the company is clearly present
4. User-provided event details

If none of these exists, stop before building the event page. Ask for an event source or present the blocker. Do not invent dates, speakers, sponsors, venue, registration deadlines, or agenda details.

Required decisions:

- Event source and source label
- Audience and registration or meeting-booking goal
- Event promise and reason to attend
- Agenda, session, speaker, resource, or recap proof that is source-supported
- CTA strategy: register, book a meeting, watch, read recap, download, or talk to the team
- Follow-up path for people who cannot attend, when supported by source material

Quality gates:

- The event details are source-supported and current enough for the intended use.
- The event page does not feel like a generic landing page with an event label pasted on it.
- Date, location, speaker, agenda, and registration language are omitted unless verified.
- CTA labels match the actual action.
- If the event is past, frame the page as recap, follow-up, or content engagement instead of pretending registration is open.

## Side-By-Side Pack Review

After all three local drafts exist, compare them together:

- Do the three pages have distinct buyer jobs?
- Does each page use source-supported proof instead of the same generic proof wall?
- Are the headlines and CTAs specific to the page type?
- Does the event page have real event gravity?
- Does the one-to-one page feel account-specific?
- Does the campaign page feel broadly launchable without becoming generic?
- Would a buyer trust each page as a serious vendor-owned experience?

Reject and revise any page that feels like a template, repeats another page's argument, or exposes production rationale.
